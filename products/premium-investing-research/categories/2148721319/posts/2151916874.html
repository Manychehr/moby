Where Americans Moved During The Pandemic & How To Play It

Published on August 14, 2021

We are in the middle of a mass migration of people from major cities to other areas of the country. The states that saw the largest influx of people were Arizona, Nevada, Texas & Florida. The states that saw the biggest outflow of people were California, Washington, Illinois, NY & Washington DC. But the biggest question we have as investors are: 1) Are these permanent and will the trends reverse? 2) If they are permanent, what short term effects will there be? And 3) What are the long term effects of this massive change in the way people live!

Let's break down the answers to these questions and which companies stand to benefit the most from this trend!

Sneak Peak for the below: One of the stocks is Lennar (LEN)

 

Overview:

Let's start off by really giving context on what occurred this last year. More than 1 in 10 Americans moved in 2020. Most of the people who moved were wealthy and ended up driving home prices up over 20% in 2020.

Major cities also lost the most people to rural and suburban areas since 2016. For example we saw the Hudson Valley (popular NY suburban/rural area) bring on 20K more full time residents. While neighborhoods like Tribeca, Dumbo, UES, all saw migration out.

Across states, we saw that 64,000 people moved from NY to FL and California dropped in population for the first time since their founding in 1850!

110,000 people moved from LA to San Diego and other areas such as Dallas, Las Vegas & Phoenix became hotspots. The underlying trend to this all is the ability to work from home.

The major cities most Americans left

So, what happens going forward?

If so many people left their homes and moved elsewhere, does this become permanent? Does the trend reverse or does it get amplified? Our takeaway is likely a bit of both.

The actual migration of people out of cities is not a fluke. The reason is because first time buyers bought nearly one third of all homes sold in 2020. With millennials driving a large portion of that (as they age out of the cities) this change is not reversing anytime soon. This is because there are 5,000,000 more 25-34 years olds than there were 10 years ago and de-urbanization has been a trend going on for years now.

The other side of this argument is the re-opening of offices. While some jobs will be remote plenty will be in person. For the people working there, they are left with two options: 1) Quit and find a new job or 2) Come back home. Assuming a 50/50 split between the choices, there still will be a lot of people who need to move back home.

But the conclusion here is clear:

No matter where people settle, the key is that people will continue to move.

Therefore we arrived at a few investment choices:

 

Investment Choices:

  • A partially risky choice is to invest in real estate of emerging areas. However, this can be hard. Real Estate company Refin estimates that 63% of offers that took place last year happened without seeing the property in person. This speaks to how competitive the market is. With home prices up so much, you would also be overpaying for an emerging city that could end up being a big bust! While there is a ton of upside, we recommend this for the investor who has experience in Real Estate and has a lot of capital.
  • The other more liquid option is to invest in stocks. The stocks we are looking at in particular are ones that facilitate mobility. If people are going to be moving, there are many companies that aid in that mobility. Let's discuss our favorite three below.

The major areas most Americans moved too

Top 3 Stocks To Play The Mobility Trend:

  • West Fraser Timber (WFG):

Wood is obviously a major input to home building across the world. With home building up so much, companies that produce the lumber itself stand to benefit. And thanks to the recent shortages in the United States, there's been even more lumber brought in from Canada (4.5% YoY import increase). This increase led to WFG netting in $2.3B in sales in Q1 with earnings sky rocketing as well!

  • Lennar (LEN):

Lennar is a home construction and real estate company. In 2017, the company was the largest home construction company in the United States after its purchase of CalAtlantic Homes. With the stock up over 50% in the last year, this trend has already been spotted. But we don't think the building of new homes is going to stop any time soon!

  • United Parcel Service, Inc. (UPS):

The least risky name on this list, UPS is serving a vital point in the mobility supply chain in order to shift goods across the world and country. If you want to participate in the upside of this trend, while also limiting your downside, this is one of the best ways to do it. While we do not expect significant upside, UPS volume's will not be shrinking anytime soon.
